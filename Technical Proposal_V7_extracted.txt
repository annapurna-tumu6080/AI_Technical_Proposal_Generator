1.1.1 Interpretation of the objectives in the TORs, critical examination of tasks 
The RECAP4NDC Project outlined in the Terms of Reference (TOR) aims to develop an 
integrated Monitoring, Evaluation, and Reporting (MER) system for Forest Landscape 
Restoration (FLR) initiatives in selected landscapes across India. The central objective is to 
design and pilot scalable, participatory, and evidence-based MER frameworks, along with 
digital tools, that can support forest restoration planning and reporting at state and national 
levels. This will be accomplished through co-development of standardized MER indicators, 
methods, and tools; application of modern technologies such as remote sensing, GIS, drones, 
IoT, and AI; and stakeholder-driven institutional integration. 
A key focus of the project is on creating MER systems that are aligned with India’s national 
priorities and international commitments, such as the Bonn Challenge, UN SDGs, Paris 
Agreement, State of Forest Reports, and National Forest Inventory. By designing standardized 
frameworks and protocols, the project seeks to enable consistent, comparable, and outcome-
focused monitoring across diverse restoration contexts. Additionally, the assignment requires 
integration with existing national and sub-national forest governance systems to ensure 
uptake and sustainability. 
Another significant goal is the development and deployment of digital tools including mobile 
applications, dashboards, and GIS platforms to collect, visualize, and analyze restoration data. 
These tools should support real-time data collection, remote validation, and interactive 
reporting for various stakeholders, from field-level practitioners to state and central 
policymakers. The digital solutions must be user-friendly, flexible for use in different 
landscapes, and interoperable with existing systems, enabling seamless data exchange and 
institutional adoption. 
Furthermore, the assignment emphasizes piloting the MER framework and tools in selected 
landscapes across Gujarat, Maharashtra, Uttarakhand, and Delhi NCR. Field testing and 
refinement will be critical to ensure that the systems are functional, scalable, and context-
appropriate. The pilots will demonstrate how MER frameworks and digital tools can track 
ecological outcomes (e.g., forest cover, soil health), social outcomes (e.g., livelihoods, 
participation), and institutional outcomes (e.g., policy alignment, stakeholder coordination) of 
restoration interventions. 
The assignment also underscores the need for participatory development of all outputs. This 
includes engaging key national and sub-national stakeholders such as MoEFCC, FSI, SFDs, 
IUCN, GIZ, and restoration practitioners through interviews, consultations, and workshops. 
This participatory approach ensures contextual relevance, capacity building, and stakeholder 
ownership of the MER systems and tools. 
In addition, the project calls for strong knowledge management and capacity-building 
components. This includes developing user manuals, SOPs, and training modules for field 
staff and government officials. Capacity-building efforts such as training-of-trainers programs 
and regional workshops will be necessary to build long-term institutional capacity to use and 
manage the MER systems.

The logical system architecture is divided into six following layers. 
1. 
User Interface Layer (Presentation Layer): This layer is responsible for interacting 
with the user by showing the website. The presentation layer then intern interacts 
with the business logic layer. The presentation layer includes the Platform interface. 
2. 
Service Orchestration Layer: This layer manages how requests and data flow 
between users and backend systems. 
i. 
Service Routing: Handles the flow of user requests to backend modules, 
ensuring that queries (e.g., analytics, data upload) are directed to appropriate 
microservices. It maintains scalability and prevents performance bottlenecks in 
high-load scenarios. 
ii. 
API Gateway: Facilitates seamless integration with external systems like 
Bhuvan, VEDAS, National FLR Portals, and state-level Management 
Information Systems. It ensures secure, standardized data exchange using 
RESTful APIs and OGC-compliant protocols. 
iii. App Routing for Local MIS Systems: This module links the FLR system to 
existing state applications (e.g., Forest MIS, Soil & Water Mission dashboards), 
enabling bidirectional data sync & updates for regional planning and reporting. 
 
3. 
Common Access Services – Platform Management: This layer governs security, 
access control, system integrity, and system-wide communication mechanisms, 
ensuring compliance with specified governance and integration requirements. 
i. 
Authorization & Authentication: Implements multi-tier user verification 
protocols, with OAuth 2.0 standards and token-based access. Supports 
differentiated login interfaces for national (MoEFCC), state (SFDs), district 
(RFOs), and community-based users. Data access is governed by role and 
geographic scope. Ensures secure access to sensitive geospatial and 
institutional datasets. This emphasizes interoperability with national databases 
and secure access, both addressed here. 
ii. 
Role-Based Access Control (RBAC): Ensures that users interact only with 
features and data aligned with their roles. For instance, RFOs can upload field 
data but not approve reports; State MIS officers can edit and validate; MoEFCC 
can analyze national rollups. Permissions are adjustable by system 
administrators. This supports institutional capacity-building and tiered data 
governance across AFOLU sectors. 
iii. Logging & Audit Trails: Tracks all system events such as user logins, data 
uploads, metadata edits, and report approvals. Maintains secure and 
timestamped logs for each transaction, ensuring traceability and compliance. 
Logs are periodically reviewed for anomalies or unauthorized actions. This 
module supports transparency and audit readiness as required in donor- and 
government-funded initiatives.. 
iv. Messaging & Notifications: Enables real-time alerts to users for pending 
validations, conflict resolution, degraded area detection, or restoration status 
updates. Alerts can be configured by priority, geographic scope, and user group. 
Delivered via email, SMS, or in-app messages. This function supports

System Integration APIs: Provides standardized APIs for real-time data 
exchange with external systems like Bhuvan, VEDAS, state forest MIS, and 
community dashboards. Ensures compliance with OGC/INSPIRE standards for

ROAM). This ensures that the system builds upon existing AFOLU data

Microservices Layer: This layer powers the core functionality of the FLR

i. 
User Management & Authentication: This module enables secure onboarding

verification, and password reset. Role-based privileges are assigned (e.g., read-
only for researchers, full access for state officers). System admins can 
deactivate or suspend users to manage platform security. 
ii. 
Data Ingestion & Integration: Enables ingestion of data from multiple sources 
such as field-based mobile apps, satellite APIs (e.g., Bhuvan, SEPAL, VEDAS),

maintained by GSRLM and the Government of Gujarat. Key integration

Sync SHG/VO data with existing livelihood program databases.

Integrate with SMS gateways and IVRS systems already used by state 
departments. 
iii. Spatial Data Management: Processes geospatial layers (raster and vector) for

NDVI, NDWI, EVI, NBR indices derived from Sentinel-2, Landsat, and 
PlanetScope data. Supports overlays with forest boundaries, working plans, and 
administrative units. Automates spatial joins, zonal stats, and time-series 
change detection. Enables preparation of maps for reporting under SDG 15.3

1.2.2 Strategy for establishing cooperation and then cooperating with the relevant actors 
To ensure effective implementation of the FLR monitoring system, the cooperation strategy 
will be grounded in participatory principles, institutional coordination, and technical validation. 
Cooperation will be achieved through a structured engagement strategy that aligns with the 
multi-stakeholder architecture involving central and state forest departments, GIS institutions, 
research bodies, community-based organizations, and enforcement agencies. 
The process will begin with a comprehensive stakeholder mapping and institutional 
assessment during the inception phase, identifying mandates, data roles, and institutional 
capacities. Based on this, tailored engagement plans will be formulated for each actor group. 
Regular multi-level stakeholder consultations and co-design workshops will facilitate 
expectation setting, capture user needs &validate tools and frameworks under development. 
A user-centric design methodology will be applied where stakeholders actively contribute to 
the design, piloting, and refinement of digital tools and MER protocols. Interactions will be 
formalized through the establishment of FLR Monitoring Committees at national, state, and 
district levels, serving as coordination and escalation platforms. These committees will provide 
governance oversight, approve workflows, and promote cross-sector alignment. 
To operationalize cooperation, the project will deploy role-specific training programs for field 
staff, GIS experts, policy planners, and community monitors. These trainings will be 
supplemented with e-learning platforms, job aids, and in-field mentorship support. Real-time 
communication channels including mobile-based alert systems, helpdesks, and feedback 
dashboards will be used to foster bi-directional coordination, especially between field actors 
and supervisory staff. 
A performance monitoring and feedback loop will be institutionalized through regular review of 
user analytics, field data consistency checks, and stakeholder satisfaction surveys. Insights 
gathered will feed into iterative tool development, adaptive management decisions, and 
institutional learning. 
Through this collaborative and adaptive cooperation strategy, the project will build shared 
ownership, ensure data quality, and institutionalize an integrated FLR monitoring system 
aligned with national reporting priorities and field-level realities.

for forest officers, technical staff, and data analysts. These will be complemented by easy-to-
understand user manuals, SOPs, and video tutorials in English and Gujarati. The system will 
include a feedback and ticketing mechanism enabling users to report issues and suggest 
improvements. Regular system audits, performance reviews, and update cycles will ensure 
that the digital tools remain effective, user-friendly, and aligned with evolving field 
requirements. 
To reduce long-term operational costs, the platform will be hosted on cost-efficient cloud 
infrastructure (e.g., AWS, Azure, or NIC/GovCloud) with minimal hardware dependency. The 
system will be designed to integrate with the existing IT infrastructure of the Forest 
Department, promoting resource sharing and avoiding duplication of services. A maintenance 
and support budget will be outlined to cover system updates, bug fixes, and user support. 
GISFY will also advise on potential funding avenues, such as convergence with state digital 
forestry programs and climate adaptation funds, to support continued operations. 
The digital tools developed will be fully institutionalized within the workflows of the Forest and 
Environment Department, ensuring ownership, accountability, and continuity. The project will 
be aligned with state-level forest working plans, FLR targets, and national afforestation 
missions under the Paris Agreement and NDC framework. Furthermore, GISFY will facilitate 
engagement with academic institutions, NGOs, and knowledge partners to promote inter-
agency collaboration and support future expansions, research-driven enhancements, and 
integration into policy decision-making cycles. 
Through this comprehensive sustainability framework, the project will not only enhance the 
current digital capabilities of the Gujarat Forest Department but also lay a strong foundation 
for scaling, institutionalizing, and evolving a resilient forest monitoring and restoration system 
that supports Gujarat’s climate and biodiversity commitments in the long run.
